Makers

Cory Doctorow, the author of Makers, specializes in writing books that are Science Fiction and is a journalist and activist alongside that. Some of his best titles are Little Brother, Walkaway and Pirate Cinema. His website is at craphound.com and is also a blogger at boingboing.net. He is also visiting professors at the University of South Carolina's School of Library and Information Science, also a notable activist and founder of the UK Open Rights group. You'll find in a lot of the books he writes the characters do actively exercise and support their rights to win. Abandoned Walmarts, discarded Elmo toys, and running from AK-47’s is what makes Makers a very interesting read. Perry and Lester are two garage inventors, who make things with probably the weirdest medium; old Elmo toys that never got off the shelves. Things such as toasters or even a car. The business of manufacturing with Elmo toys is booming, until one day, it all collapses. The New Work, the story setting’s business capital, just stopped. Lester and Perry build amusement rides in abandoned Walmarts to commemorate the late New Work, and then end up in a legal mess with a Disney executive. This book was, quite frankly, a wild ride. It seemed more like a Ray Bradbury book than it did a Cory Doctorow book, which quite heavily emphasises civil rights, where as this one is some crazy scenario which has two goofy guys as the main characters. Now, this isn’t bad at all, just not something I expected from Doctorow. Typically, they have an intense dystopian society theme to it. This book does have that, but it’s nothing really actually engaging. Overall though, a pretty good book.

AoW 8 “Having this writing system, you can teach kids how to speak Fulani just like you teach them to speak English. It will help preserve the language and let people be creative and innovative.” Microsoft Story Labs have provided an African society with a new writing system that helps combat illiteracy. It’s called ADLaM, and the system has only just began it’s addition to Unicode, and it has a movement through social media. ADLaM and the alphabet were created by two brothers named Ibrahima and Abdoulaye Barry, from Guinea, and wanted to create an alphabet for their native language called Fulfulde. The process went like this: They shut themselves in a room and mindlessly drew shapes on a paper with their eyes shut. Then, they’d individually go through each shape and decide which one they liked best for all the sounds. This is what became known as the ADLaM system. Eventually, they bring coverage to Microsoft, and MS makes it happen. “We just wanted people to be able to write correctly in their own language, but we didn’t know what that meant. We didn’t know how much work it would be. If we knew everything we would have to go through, I don’t think we would have done it.” Illiteracy was a notable issue in the community, so this was a big improvement. The Fulbhe were nomadists who generally settled in West Africa, more specifically in Sudan to Senegal. “ADLaM is an acronym that translates to ‘the alphabet that will prevent a people from being lost’” This is moving. Two brothers pushed for a missing piece in their society that was much needed. With the help of Microsoft and modern technology, they were able to make it a reality. It’s quite interesting, the process of encoding fonts. Individual letters are not represented by pictures, but by pure raw data themselves. I’m studying Computer Science and fonts are something I just can’t grasp yet. A TrueType font file is what stores it, but I don’t know what other component of the operating system parses it. AoW #5

Large corporations suffer from failure all the time. All over in the news, you see scandalous operations from these companies. While it may seem all they get from it is consequences, there’s still a high amount of benefit from them, as well. Do you know how everyone has told you that you learn from your failures? That applies to just about everyone and everything else as well. When companies mess up, they learn from their failures and ultimately succeed afterward — the CEO of Coca Cola Co. James Quincy pushed for his employees to overcome the fear of failure because he knew that failure wasn’t that much of a big deal. “If we’re not making mistakes, we’re not trying hard enough.” Netflix CEO Reed Hastings worried that his service was offering too many new hit shows and canceling too many original and loved ones. “We have to take more risk, to try more crazy things, we should have a higher cancel rate overall.” Not all companies learn from failure, though. Everyone knows what happened with Mark Zuckerberg, possibly the most influential and well-known scandal so far. Zuckerberg was found selling Facebook users’ data to third-party and malicious sources. Huawei’s telecommunications department was being used as a gateway to send data of American citizens to the Chinese government. Google, the provider of the mobile operating system, cut off Huawei’s usage of their services, and Huawei was boycotted from trade in the U.S. You only learn from failure if you care to, and try.
